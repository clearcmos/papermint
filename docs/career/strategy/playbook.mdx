## LinkedIn Positioning

No, your headline does **not** need to match your current job title. The consensus is clear:

**Your headline is a marketing tool, not an HR record.** Your *Experience section* is where your actual title goes. The headline is where you position yourself for the roles you want.

Key points from what experts recommend:

- **Use your headline for what recruiters search for.** If recruiters search "Infrastructure Architect" and your headline says "Senior IT Specialist," you're invisible. The headline is how LinkedIn's search algorithm finds you.
- **Your Experience section carries the official title.** Put "Senior IT Specialist" there with a description of what you actually do. No conflict, no dishonesty.
- **The headline should describe what you do, not what HR calls you.** Multiple sources say it's "perfectly acceptable to create your own title that describes the bigger picture of what you do."
- **Align it with where you're going, not where you've been.** Especially if you're open to new roles (which you are), the headline should match the job postings you want to show up in.

**Recommended headline:**

> **Automation & Identity Architect | API, Okta, Terraform, GCP, Google Workspace & Kandji**

Your Experience section keeps "Senior IT Specialist" as-is. No one will bat an eye.

## Salary Negotiation

Tactical anchoring strategy for a specific opportunity.

### Ask for 150k base.

**Why 150k specifically:**

1. **Round number, natural anchor.** It doesn't look calculated or like you have insider info. "I'd need 150" rolls off the tongue. Compare to 147k which is oddly specific and might signal you know something.

2. **The boss gets to "win."** When you say 150k and he counters at 145k, he feels like he negotiated you down. You feel like you got what you wanted. Everyone walks away satisfied. This is the fundamental mechanic of anchoring — you're manufacturing a concession that costs you nothing.

3. **It's not high enough to kill the deal.** The boss privately floated 145k+5% bonus (total comp ~152k). Your ask of 150k base is *within the envelope he already mentally committed to*. There's zero chance he walks away over a 150k ask when he was prepared for 152k total comp.

4. **It's consistent with the "hard to get" framing.** Your friend told his boss it would be difficult to recruit you. Asking for 150k reinforces that narrative. Asking for 145k (or less) contradicts it — why would someone who's "hard to get" come at exactly what they were prepared to offer?

5. **If he just says yes, you got 150k.** Low probability but nonzero. You'd leave 5k on the table by opening at 145k.

### What NOT to do:

- **Don't say 145k.** You're right — it anchors at or below. The boss's instinct will be to negotiate down from whatever you say. If you say 145k, you might land at 140k. That's not what you want.
- **Don't say 155k+.** That starts to feel like a stretch beyond what the friend's hype can support, and risks the boss recalibrating his expectations about the difficulty of hiring you.
- **Don't disclose your current salary.** The jump from 105k to 150k (~43%) could spook him into thinking he's overpaying. Your current comp is irrelevant — the question is what you're worth to *his* organization. If asked, deflect: "I'm focused on what makes sense for this role and what I'd need to make a move."
- **Don't mention the bonus first.** Let them bring up the bonus structure. If you say "150k base," and they come back with "how about 145k with a 5% bonus," that's a win — you got their ceiling plus they think they negotiated.

### Why the boss won't walk away:

Your read is correct. He has a uniquely low-risk hire in front of him — someone his trusted employee has vouched for personally over 25 years. That kind of interpersonal trust is impossible to recruit for on the open market. He knows that. The difference between 145k and 150k (~$5k/year, ~$400/month) is noise compared to the value of a guaranteed cultural fit reporting to someone who already knows how to work with them.

**Bottom line:** Say 150k. Land at 145k. Treat the bonus as gravy.
